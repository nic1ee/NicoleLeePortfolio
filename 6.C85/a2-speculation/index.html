Exploratory Visual Analysis: Corporate Ownership & Vacancy in Boston (2004–2024)

Vis & Society 2026 — A2 Exploratory Visual Analysis
Subtheme: Speculation (MAPC “Homes for Profit”)
Author: Nicole Lee — Date: 2026-02-24

This report explores neighborhood-level trends in corporate ownership and occupancy in Boston over the last 20 years. I focus on (1) where corporate ownership is most concentrated today, (2) how patterns have changed since 2004, and (3) whether corporate ownership is associated with neighborhood context, including racial composition and housing vacancy.

Initial Questions (Motivation)

  • Q1: Where is corporate ownership most concentrated in 2024? Motivation: “Homes for Profit” argues investor activity is unevenly distributed; I wanted to identify which neighborhoods have the highest rates today.
  • Q2: Which neighborhoods saw the biggest growth in corporate ownership from 2004 to 2024? Motivation: The report emphasizes increasing investor presence over time; I wanted to see whether growth is citywide or concentrated in specific areas.
  • Q3: Is corporate ownership associated with neighborhood demographics or vacancy in 2024? Motivation: “Homes for Profit” discusses displacement pressures and investor strategies; vacancy may reflect turnover and/or units held off-market.

Data: Corporate ownership & owner-occupancy rates by Boston neighborhood over time (2004–2024), plus census-derived counts for population and housing units.

Main Takeaways

  • Corporate ownership in 2024 is highest in a small set of dense, central neighborhoods, but rates vary meaningfully even within the “top” group.
  • From 2004–2024, corporate ownership rises across all Boston neighborhoods, suggesting broad growth rather than a single-neighborhood effect.
  • In 2024, corporate ownership is not strongly determined by neighborhood racial composition, but it shows a clearer positive association with vacancy.
  • High corporate ownership + high vacancy outliers may reflect investor behavior or housing stock dynamics worth deeper investigation in a final project.

Notes on transformations: I computed nonwhite_share = 1 - (white_all / tot_pop_all) and vacancy_rate = vacant_unit / tot_unit. I compared corporate ownership rates between 2004 and 2024 by merging neighborhood records and computing the difference.
