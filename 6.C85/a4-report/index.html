Final Reflection

This assignment made it very clear how easily the same dataset can support multiple interpretations depending on visualization choices. When I first calculated the substantiation rates, I assumed the results would lead to a straightforward conclusion. However, once I began building and iterating on the visualizations, I realized how much interpretation happens before a chart is ever shown. Decisions about filtering, grouping, axis scaling, and even the wording of titles all shape the story the viewer ultimately sees. This directly connects to ideas discussed in lecture about framing and visual rhetoric, where the same data can lead to very different interpretations depending on how it is presented.

One surprising part of the process was how often I had to revisit my assumptions about the data itself. Early on, I noticed that my calculation for substantiated cases produced results that seemed unusually high, which forced me to step back and double check how I was grouping the Board Disposition field. That debugging process made me more aware of how easily small data transformation decisions can affect the narrative of a visualization. The dataset also includes categories like Unknown or Refused for complainant ethnicity, which raised questions about whether those records should be included, filtered out, or treated separately. I ultimately chose to keep them visible in the more neutral visualization because removing them would have artificially simplified the dataset.

Throughout the design process I experimented with several ways to make the visualizations more visually striking. At one point I considered adding icons or illustrative images next to the categories to make the groups feel more distinct. While those additions made the visualization more eye catching, they also began to feel overly persuasive and somewhat distracting from the data itself. I also experimented with stronger highlighting and more aggressive annotations, but those versions started to feel noisy and less trustworthy. Because of this, I intentionally simplified the final design and relied more on layout and framing rather than decorative elements. This iterative process helped me better understand how easily design choices can shift from clarification into persuasion.

Working through both versions of the visualization helped me think more carefully about what ethical analysis and visualization means in practice. I do not think ethical visualization means eliminating persuasion entirely, since every chart requires choices about what comparisons to highlight. However, the boundary seems to appear when design choices remove context that viewers would reasonably expect to see. For example, filtering to only the highest and lowest groups can technically be accurate, but it becomes misleading if the viewer assumes those are the only categories that exist. In contrast, showing the full distribution and using a consistent scale provides more context even if it produces a less dramatic story. This assignment helped me see how thin the line can be between persuasive communication and misleading representation, and how important it is to be transparent about the decisions that shape a visualization.
