Nicole Lee — nhlee@mit.edu

Proposition: NYPD complaint substantiation rates vary substantially by complainant ethnicity.

FOR the Proposition

A 12-percentage-point gap in substantiation rates

Dataset: Civilian Complaints Against New York City Police Officers (ProPublica)

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Score -1.5: I filtered the visualization to show only the highest- and lowest-substantiation ethnicity categories instead of displaying the full distribution. This makes the difference appear more dramatic because the viewer immediately focuses on the extremes. While the values themselves are correct, removing the intermediate groups hides the broader context of the dataset.
  • Score -1.0: The title frames the gap using relative language (“50% greater than the lowest”). This is a framing technique discussed in class, where the same numbers can feel more significant depending on how they are described. Presenting the difference as a relative percentage emphasizes magnitude more strongly than stating the raw difference alone.
  • Score -1.5: The y-axis is zoomed to the observed range (roughly 20–40%). This visually amplifies the distance between the points. If the axis extended from 0–100%, the gap would appear much smaller and less visually dramatic.
  • Score +0.5: I included a reference line showing the overall average substantiation rate. This provides some contextual grounding for the viewer, allowing them to see whether each group sits above or below the typical rate.
  • Score +1.0: I annotated the values directly on the chart. Even though the design emphasizes a persuasive comparison, the numerical values remain visible and verifiable to the viewer.

AGAINST the Proposition

Substantiation rates are similar across complainant ethnicities

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Score +1.5: I included all complainant ethnicity categories rather than highlighting only the extremes. This allows viewers to see the full distribution and recognize that most categories cluster fairly closely together.
  • Score +1.0: The axis runs from 0–100%. Using a full scale avoids exaggerating differences and aligns with visualization conventions discussed in lecture that help charts appear more neutral and interpretable.
  • Score +0.5: The subtitle explicitly states that the spread is about a 12-percentage-point band. This frames the interpretation while still referencing the actual quantitative range in the dataset.
  • Score +1.0: I used a single consistent color across categories. This avoids visually privileging any one group and keeps the focus on comparing magnitudes rather than implying a hierarchy.
  • Score +2.0: I retained categories like Unknown, Refused, and Null instead of removing them. Including them reflects the dataset more honestly and acknowledges the uncertainty present in real-world data collection.

Final Reflection

This assignment made it very clear how easily the same dataset can support multiple interpretations depending on visualization choices. When I first calculated the substantiation rates, I expected the results to be relatively straightforward. However, once I began experimenting with different chart designs, it became obvious that small design decisions — such as filtering categories, choosing axis scales, or framing titles — could dramatically change the narrative the visualization communicated. This connects closely to concepts we discussed in lecture about how visualizations are never completely neutral. Even when the data remains identical, the way it is structured and presented can strongly influence how viewers interpret the results.

During the design process I went through many iterations where I experimented with additional visual elements that could make the comparison more striking. At one point I considered adding icons or small illustrative images near the points to make the categories feel more visually distinct. We discussed in class how annotations and visual cues can guide interpretation, and initially I thought this might strengthen the story of the chart. However, once I implemented those ideas the visualization started to feel overly persuasive and somewhat untrustworthy. The added illustrations also made the figure feel noisy and distracted from the data itself. Because of this, I intentionally removed those elements and simplified the final version. This iterative process helped me better understand the boundary between persuasive communication and misleading design.

Overall, I now think ethical data visualization is less about avoiding persuasion entirely and more about maintaining transparency about what the data actually shows. Some persuasive framing is unavoidable, since every visualization requires decisions about scale, comparison, and emphasis. However, a visualization becomes misleading when those choices remove important context or exaggerate relationships that would appear smaller if viewers saw the full distribution. Working through multiple design iterations helped me recognize how easily a chart can cross that line, and it reinforced the importance of clearly presenting the data while still being thoughtful about how visual framing affects interpretation.
