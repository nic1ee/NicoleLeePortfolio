Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Nicole Lee, nhlee@mit.edu

Proposition: NYPD complaint substantiation rates vary substantially by complainant ethnicity.

FOR the Proposition

A 12-percentage-point gap in substantiation rates

Dataset: Civilian Complaints Against New York City Police Officers (ProPublica)

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Score -1.5: I filtered the visualization to show only the highest and lowest substantiation categories rather than displaying the full distribution of ethnic groups. This focuses attention on the largest gap and makes it appear like the central story of the data, even though other categories fall between these two values. The decision increases visual impact but hides important context.
  • Score -1.0: The title frames the comparison as a “12-percentage-point gap” and emphasizes the relative difference between groups. Concepts discussed in lecture highlight how titles can shape interpretation before viewers even examine the marks in a chart. Using relative language makes the difference appear more dramatic than simply listing the raw values.
  • Score -1.5: The y-axis is truncated to roughly the observed range of the data rather than extending from 0 to 100 percent. This visually exaggerates the distance between the two values, making the difference appear larger than it would on a full scale.
  • Score +0.5: I included a reference line showing the overall average substantiation rate. This provides some contextual grounding for the viewer and allows the points to be interpreted relative to a baseline.
  • Score +1.0: I added direct annotations for the percentages. Even though the visualization emphasizes a particular comparison, the actual numbers remain visible and verifiable.

AGAINST the Proposition

Substantiation rates are similar across complainant ethnicities

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Score +1.5: I included all ethnicity categories rather than filtering to only the extremes. This allows viewers to see the full distribution of the dataset and observe that most values cluster fairly closely together.
  • Score +1.0: The y-axis extends from 0 to 100 percent. Using a full axis scale avoids exaggerating the visual distance between values and aligns with visualization conventions discussed in lecture that make charts easier to interpret accurately.
  • Score +0.5: The subtitle explicitly states that the spread is about a twelve percentage point band. This still guides interpretation but does so using a description grounded directly in the observed data.
  • Score +1.0: I used a single consistent color across categories rather than a diverging palette. This avoids visually prioritizing one group over another and keeps the comparison neutral.
  • Score +2.0: I retained categories such as Unknown, Refused, and Null rather than filtering them out. While this makes the visualization slightly messier, it better reflects the real structure of the dataset and avoids artificially simplifying the data.

Final Reflection

This assignment made it very clear how easily the same dataset can support multiple interpretations depending on visualization choices. When I first calculated the substantiation rates, I assumed the results would lead to a straightforward conclusion. However, once I began building and iterating on the visualizations, I realized how much interpretation happens before a chart is ever shown. Decisions about filtering, grouping, axis scaling, and even the wording of titles all shape the story the viewer ultimately sees. This connects directly to ideas discussed in lecture about visual framing and rhetorical choices in visualization, where the same data can communicate very different narratives depending on how it is presented.

One surprising part of the process was how often I had to revisit assumptions about the data itself. Early on, my calculation for substantiated cases produced results that seemed unusually high, which forced me to step back and double check how I was grouping the Board Disposition field. That debugging process made me more aware of how easily data transformation decisions can affect the story a visualization tells. The dataset also includes categories such as Unknown or Refused for complainant ethnicity, which raised questions about whether those records should be included, filtered, or treated separately. I ultimately chose to keep them visible in the more neutral visualization because removing them would have simplified the dataset in a way that could hide uncertainty.

Throughout the design process I experimented with several ways to make the visualizations more visually striking. At one point I considered adding icons or small illustrations next to the categories to make the groups more visually distinct. While those additions made the chart more visually engaging, they also began to feel overly persuasive and somewhat distracting from the data itself. I also experimented with stronger highlighting and additional annotations, but those versions started to feel noisy and less trustworthy. Because of this, I simplified the final designs and relied more on layout and framing rather than decorative elements. This iterative process helped me understand how easily visualization design can shift from clarification into persuasion.

Working through both versions of the visualization helped me think more carefully about what ethical analysis and visualization means in practice. Ethical visualization does not necessarily mean eliminating persuasion, since every chart requires choices about which comparisons to emphasize. However, the boundary seems to appear when design choices remove context that viewers would reasonably expect to see. For example, filtering to only the highest and lowest categories is technically accurate, but it becomes misleading if viewers assume those are the only groups that exist. Showing the full distribution and using a consistent axis scale provides more context even if it produces a less dramatic story. This assignment helped me see how thin the line can be between persuasive communication and misleading representation, and how important it is to be transparent about the decisions that shape a visualization.

Dataset

ProPublica, Civilian Complaints Against New York City Police Officers.
